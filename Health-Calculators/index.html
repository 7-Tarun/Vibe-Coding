What is BMI & How is it Calculated?

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a health screening test which uses height and weight measurements of an individual in order to determine the total body fat percentage. The BMI is obtained by dividing the weight in kilograms with the square of the height in meters (\(kg/m^2\)), which then classifies people according to different weight categories.

The Metric Formula:
BMI = Weight (kg) / (Height (m) × Height (m))

Example: If you weight 70 kg and are 175 cm (1.75 m) tall, your BMI is calculated as 70 / (1.75 × 1.75) = 22.86.

Standard BMI Categories

The World Health Organization (WHO) classifies BMI ranges as follows:

Category BMI Range
Underweight Less than 18.5
Normal weight 18.5 - 24.9
Overweight 25.0 - 29.9
Obesity (Class I) 30.0 - 34.9
Obesity (Class II & III) 35.0 and above

Frequently Asked Questions

1. Is BMI completely accurate for everyone?

While BMI is a useful general indicator, it has limitations. It does not distinguish between muscle and fat mass. For example, athletes or bodybuilders may have a high BMI due to heavy muscle mass, not excess body fat.

2. Does age or gender affect BMI calculations?

The standard adult BMI formula applies equally to men and women over the age of 20. However, for children and teenagers, BMI is interpreted differently using age- and gender-specific percentiles.

3. What is a healthy BMI for older adults?

For adults over 65, a slightly higher BMI (between 25 and 27) might be healthier and is sometimes associated with a lower risk of mortality and better protection against bone thinning.

4. How can I lower my BMI safely?

Lowering your BMI requires a consistent calorie deficit. This is best achieved through a combination of a balanced, whole-food diet, regular cardiovascular exercise, and strength training. Always consult a healthcare provider before making drastic changes.

5. Are BMI and Body Fat Percentage the same thing?

No. BMI is just a ratio of your height to your weight. It guesses your health status. Body Fat Percentage calculates the exact amount of fat tissue on your body, making it a much more accurate metric for true fitness.

What Are Macronutrients?

Macronutrients are the three main nutrients your body needs in large amounts: protein, carbohydrates, and fat. Each plays a unique role in energy, recovery, and overall performance.

Macro Conversion Formulas

Frequently Asked Questions

1. How do I choose the right macro split?

Choose a split based on your goal: balanced for general health, high protein for muscle support, low carb for fat loss, and keto for a very low-carb lifestyle.

2. Can I change macros daily?

Yes. You can adjust your macros based on training days, rest days, or how your body responds. The calculator gives you a good baseline.

3. Why do fat grams use 9 kcal per gram?

Fat contains more energy than protein or carbs. The calculator uses 9 kcal per gram of fat to convert calories into fat grams accurately.

Frequently Asked Questions

1. Is it better to drink before or after exercise?

Drink water throughout the day, especially before exercise. After exercise, rehydrate to replace fluid loss from sweat and support recovery.

2. Can I drink too much water?

Yes, excessive water intake can cause hyponatremia. Stick to a reasonable daily range and listen to your body's thirst cues.

3. Do I need more water in hot weather?

Yes. Hot and humid conditions increase sweat rate, so you should drink more water to stay hydrated and prevent overheating.

Choose protein-dense foods like lean meats, eggs, dairy, beans, and legumes. Protein shakes can also help fill gaps in your daily intake.

3. Can protein help with weight loss?

Yes. Protein increases fullness and can help preserve muscle while you're in a calorie deficit, making it a useful tool for healthy weight loss.

Frequently Asked Questions

1. Can ideal weight vary by body type?

Yes. This calculator uses standard BMI and Devine estimates. Individual differences in muscle mass, bone density, and body shape mean your ideal weight can vary from these estimates.

2. Is ideal weight the same as healthy weight?

Not exactly. Ideal weight is a guideline, while healthy weight depends on your body composition, fitness level, and overall health. Use these ranges as a starting point, not a strict rule.

3. Should I follow the Devine formula exactly?

The Devine formula is a helpful estimate, especially for planning. However, you should adjust based on how you feel, your performance, and any guidance from your healthcare provider.

Your BMR

-- kcal/day

How BMR Works

Your Basal Metabolic Rate is the energy your body burns at rest to support vital functions like breathing, circulation, and cell repair. This is the baseline number used to build your daily calorie plan.

Mifflin-St Jeor Equation
Male: BMR = 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) − 5 × age + 5
Female: BMR = 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) − 5 × age − 161

BMR Factor Table

Factor Effect on BMR
Gender Male values are typically higher due to greater lean muscle mass.
Weight Higher weight increases total energy required at rest.
Height Taller people burn more calories because they have larger body surface area.
Age BMR decreases with age as metabolic rate slows.

Frequently Asked Questions

1. Can BMR change over time?

Yes. BMR will change with age, weight, body composition, and hormonal shifts. Recalculate it periodically, especially after major weight changes.

2. Does exercise count in BMR?

No. BMR only estimates the calories burned at rest. Exercise calories are added separately by tools like the TDEE or calorie calculator.

3. Why is BMR lower for women?

Women often have lower BMR values because they typically carry more body fat and less lean muscle mass compared to men, and muscle burns more calories than fat.

What is TDEE & How is it Calculated?

The Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) refers to the total number of calories that one’s body expends throughout a day. TDEE combines your basal metabolic rate (BMR) with calories burned during daily activity and digestion.

The Core Formula:
TDEE = BMR × Activity Multiplier

Note: This calculator first estimates your BMR using the industry-standard Mifflin-St Jeor Equation and then multiplies it by your activity level.

Standard Activity Multipliers

Your BMR is multiplied by one of these lifestyle factors to calculate your final TDEE:

Activity Level Multiplier General Description
Sedentary 1.2 Desk job, little to no formal exercise.
Lightly Active 1.375 Light daily activity, exercise 1-3 days/week.
Moderately Active 1.55 Moderate daily activity, exercise 3-5 days/week.
Very Active 1.725 Physically demanding lifestyle, heavy exercise 6-7 days/week.
Extra Active 1.9 Physical labor job or intense professional training 2x/day.

Frequently Asked Questions

1. How do I use my TDEE to lose or gain weight?

Your TDEE is the number of calories you need to maintain your current weight. For weight loss, consume fewer calories than your TDEE. For weight gain, eat more calories than your TDEE.

2. Can TDEE be used for muscle gain?

Yes. If you want to build muscle, aim for a modest calorie surplus above your TDEE while supporting your training with enough protein and resistance exercise.

3. Does this calculator work for older adults?

The formula is a good estimate for most adults, including older adults. However, as metabolism changes with age, recalculating after significant weight changes or activity shifts is helpful.

What is the U.S. Navy Method?

The U.S. Navy Method is a widely accepted algorithm that estimates your body fat percentage using standard tape measurements. It is considered one of the most accurate, accessible methods without requiring clinical equipment like DEXA scans or calipers.

The Logarithmic Formula (Metric):
Men: %Fat = 495 / (1.0324 - 0.19077 × log10(waist - neck) + 0.15456 × log10(height)) - 450 Women: %Fat = 495 / (1.29579 - 0.35004 × log10(waist + hips - neck) + 0.22100 × log10(height)) - 450

Note: Because fat distribution differs significantly by biological sex, women require a hip measurement to accurately compute the lean body mass ratio.

Body Fat Categories

The American Council on Exercise (ACE) defines the following body fat percentage categories based on gender:

Category Men Women
Essential Fat 2% - 5% 10% - 13%
Athletes 6% - 13% 14% - 20%
Fitness 14% - 17% 21% - 24%
Average 18% - 24% 25% - 31%
Obese 25% and higher 32% and higher

Frequently Asked Questions

1. Is the tape measure method (U.S. Navy) actually accurate?

While not as clinically precise as a DEXA scan or hydrostatic weighing, the U.S. Navy Method is highly effective for tracking progress over time. Consistency in exactly where and how tight you pull the measuring tape matters more than pinpoint clinical accuracy.

2. Why is my body fat percentage high even if my BMI is normal?

This is often referred to as being "skinny fat" (normal weight obesity). It occurs when you have a lower amount of muscle mass relative to your body fat. Incorporating strength training can help build muscle and improve this ratio, even if your total weight remains the same.

3. How often should I measure my body fat?

Since tape measurements can fluctuate due to daily bloating or water retention, it is best to measure once every 2 to 4 weeks. For the most reliable data, always measure under the exact same conditions—ideally in the morning, after using the restroom and before eating.
